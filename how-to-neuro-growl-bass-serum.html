How to Make a Neuro Growl Bass in Serum (Resampling Walkthrough)

The neuro growl is the talking, snarling, metallic bass that defines neurofunk and the heavier end of drum and bass. It sounds complex because it is built in layers: a moving Reese foundation, then resampling and modulation stacked on top until the bass starts to growl and speak. This guide walks you through the full process in Xfer Serum, the same approach the serious neuro producers use.

By the end you will have a growling neuro bass you can drop into a track, and you will understand the resampling loop that makes it possible to push the sound as far as you want. This sits at an intermediate level, but every step is explained plainly. Budget around fifteen to twenty minutes.

What you need before starting

  • Xfer Serum (1.3 or later, including Serum 2). Preset Drive packs are .fxp and load in every DAW.
  • A way to record audio back into your DAW (resampling), since the growl comes from processing a bounced bass.
  • A DnB-tempo project (around 172 to 174 BPM) so you can hear the bass move.
  • Estimated time: 15 to 20 minutes.

Step 1: Build a strong Reese foundation

A neuro growl starts life as a Reese. If you have not built one before, the short version is below.

  • Two saw oscillators, slightly detuned against each other for movement.
  • Dropped down into bass range.
  • Through a low-pass or band-pass filter with a touch of resonance.

You should see: a moving, snarling bass that already has character. This is your raw material.

Note: For a full breakdown of this stage, the Reese build is the natural prerequisite. Get that solid before you start resampling, because a weak Reese makes a weak growl. See the step-by-step Reese guide.

Step 2: Add formant and motion with a band-pass sweep

The vocal, talking quality of neuro bass comes from sweeping a narrow filter across the sound.

  • Switch your filter toward band-pass so only a narrow slice of frequencies passes.
  • Assign an LFO or an envelope to the filter cutoff.
  • Sweep it slowly across the held note. As the band moves, the bass starts to sound like it is forming vowels.

You should see: the bass taking on a vocal, formant-like motion. That is the neuro talk beginning.

Step 3: Introduce FM for metallic harmonics

Frequency modulation is what gives neuro bass its metallic, aggressive edge.

  • In Serum, route one oscillator to modulate another using the FM routing.
  • Bring up a small amount of FM and listen as harsh, metallic harmonics appear.
  • Modulate the FM amount with an envelope so the metallic character moves rather than sitting static.

You should see: a grittier, more complex tone with a clearly metallic component.

Step 4: Resample the bass into audio

This is the step that separates a basic bass from a real neuro growl. You bounce what you have, then treat the audio as new raw material.

  • Record or bounce a couple of bars of your current bass to an audio clip.
  • Drag that audio back into Serum's oscillator (Serum lets you import audio as a wavetable or sample source).
  • Now you are designing on top of a sound that already has movement baked in.

You should see: your processed bass loaded back into Serum, ready for a second pass of design.

Step 5: Re-modulate the resampled sound

With the movement already inside the audio, your new modulation stacks on top for complexity.

  • Add a fresh filter sweep over the resampled audio.
  • Layer a second, faster LFO for micro-movement.
  • Push the filter and FM further than you would dare on a single-pass sound, because the resampled base is already stable.

You should see: a denser, more alive growl with layers of motion interacting.

Step 6: Stack distortion and waveshaping

Neuro bass is aggressive by design. Distortion is where it bares its teeth.

  • Run the resampled growl through Serum's distortion or waveshaper.
  • Try multiple distortion types in series for more complex harmonics.
  • Automate or modulate the distortion drive so the aggression breathes with the track.

You should see: a harder, snarling bass with rich harmonic content.

Step 7: Keep the sub clean and separate

All that processing can wreck your low end. Protect it.

  • Add a clean sine sub on the root note, mono, with no distortion.
  • High-pass the growl body so it stops fighting the sub.
  • Check on headphones and a system that you have weight down low and aggression up in the mids.

You should see: a growl that is brutal in the mids and solid at the bottom.

Step 8: Resample again if you want to go further

Neuro producers often resample two or three times, each pass adding a layer.

  • Bounce your current growl.
  • Load it back into Serum and repeat the modulation and distortion stages.
  • Stop when the sound is as complex as the track needs. More is not always better.

You should now have: a finished neuro growl bass, layered and mix-ready.

Troubleshooting

  • My growl sounds harsh and thin, not heavy. You are missing the clean sub from Step 7. The aggression lives in the mids, the weight has to come from a separate sine.
  • The metallic FM is overwhelming everything. Pull the FM amount down and modulate it with an envelope so it moves instead of sitting at full blast.
  • My resampled bass sounds worse, not better. Make sure the source you bounced was already strong. Resampling amplifies whatever is there, including weaknesses.

FAQ

What is the difference between a Reese and a neuro growl?

A Reese is the detuned, moving foundation. A neuro growl is a Reese that has been resampled, FM-processed and distorted until it snarls and talks. The growl is the more advanced sound built on the Reese.

Do I have to resample to get a neuro bass?

You can get partway with FM and filter motion alone, but resampling is what gives neuro bass its signature density and complexity.
