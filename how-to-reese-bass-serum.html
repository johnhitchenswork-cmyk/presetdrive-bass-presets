How to Make a Reese Bass in Serum (Step by Step, + Ready-Made Presets)

You have heard that thick, snarling, moving bass that sits under nearly every serious drum and bass and neurofunk track. That is a Reese. It started as a single detuned sawtooth and grew into the most recognisable low end in bass music. The good news is you can build one from scratch in Xfer Serum in about ten minutes once you know the moves. The better news is that if you would rather get the sound and get back to writing the actual track, there are ready-made Reese presets waiting for you.

By the end of this guide you will have a working Reese patch in Serum that you can drop straight into a DnB or neuro project, plus the understanding to tweak it for your own tracks. This is a beginner-friendly walkthrough, no prior sound-design theory needed. It takes about ten to fifteen minutes.

What you need before starting

  • Xfer Serum (version 1.3 or later, works in every DAW). Preset Drive packs are .fxp and load in Serum and Serum 2.
  • A DAW project open at a DnB tempo (around 172 to 174 BPM) so you can hear the bass in context.
  • Optional: a MIDI clip with a low note held for a bar or two so you can hear the movement.
  • Estimated time: 10 to 15 minutes.

Step 1: Start from an init patch with two saws

Open a fresh instance of Serum and clear it back to the default init patch so nothing is fighting you.

  • Load the default init preset so Oscillator A is a clean basic shape.
  • Set Oscillator A to a saw waveform (the standard analog saw table).
  • Turn on Oscillator B and set it to the same saw waveform.

You should see: two oscillators, both running a saw, both audible when you hold a note. Right now it sounds like a plain synth. That is correct, the character comes next.

Step 2: Detune the two oscillators against each other

The heart of a Reese is two saws slightly out of tune so they beat against each other and create that slow, churning motion.

  • On Oscillator B, nudge the coarse or fine tune so it sits a few cents away from Oscillator A.
  • Keep the detune subtle at first. Small amounts give a slow, wide wobble. Large amounts get aggressive and dissonant.
  • Hold a low note and listen. You want a sound that feels like it is breathing, not one that is painfully out of tune.

You should see: a thicker, moving tone instead of a static one. This movement is the Reese signature.

Note: If it sounds too thin, add a third voice. You can detune within a single oscillator using Serum's unison, but classic Reeses come from two separate oscillators beating against each other. Start with two and only add unison if you want it wider.

Step 3: Pull the bass down into low-end territory

A Reese needs to live low to do its job under the drums.

  • Drop both oscillators down an octave or two using the octave control so the root note sits in bass range.
  • Play your held low note and check it is not muddy. If the low end feels weak, you will fix that with a sub layer in Step 6.

You should see: the patch now reads as a bass, not a mid-range lead.

Step 4: Shape the tone with the filter

The filter is where a Reese goes from a raw saw stack to a usable bass.

  • Route both oscillators through Serum's filter.
  • Choose a low-pass filter to start, then experiment with band-pass for a more vocal, mid-focused neuro tone.
  • Pull the cutoff down so the harsh top is tamed, then bring it back up until the sound has bite without being shrill.
  • Add a touch of resonance for character, but keep it controlled so it does not whistle.

You should see: a smoother, more musical bass that still keeps its movement.

Step 5: Add motion with an LFO on the filter

Static Reeses are fine, but the ones that grab attention move over time.

  • Assign an LFO to the filter cutoff.
  • Set the LFO to a slow rate, synced to your tempo, so the filter sweeps gently across a bar.
  • Keep the modulation depth modest. You want movement that supports the groove, not a wild sweep that distracts from it.

You should see: the filter slowly opening and closing, giving the bass life across each bar.

Step 6: Layer a clean sub for weight

Most of a Reese lives in the mids. To make it hit on a real system, give it a dedicated sub underneath.

  • Turn on a third oscillator (or a second Serum instance) set to a clean sine.
  • Tune the sine to the root note, one or two octaves below the Reese body.
  • Keep this sub mono and clean, with no detune, so your low end stays solid and translates to club systems.

You should see: the bass now has both movement up top and real weight at the bottom.

Step 7: Process with distortion and EQ

A Reese earns its grit through processing.

  • Add Serum's distortion or waveshaper to the Reese body (not the sub) for harmonics and aggression.
  • Use an EQ to carve out boxy low-mids and let the sub breathe.
  • High-pass the distorted body so it is not muddying your sub region, and keep the sub clean below it.

You should see: a fuller, more aggressive bass that still has a clean, controlled bottom end.

Step 8: Save it and drop it into the track

  • Save your patch in Serum so you can recall it.
  • Bounce or play it under your drums and check the balance in the full mix.
  • Adjust detune, filter motion and distortion to taste for the specific track.

You should now have: a working Reese bass in Serum, ready to anchor a DnB or neuro track.

Troubleshooting

  • My Reese sounds painfully out of tune. You have detuned too far. Pull Oscillator B's tune back toward Oscillator A until the movement is musical rather than dissonant.
  • It disappears in the mix. Your sub and your Reese body are probably fighting in the same frequency range. High-pass the body and keep the sine sub clean and mono below it.
  • It sounds static and boring. You skipped the filter LFO in Step 5. Slow, synced filter motion is what makes a Reese feel alive.
  • The presets will not load in my Serum. Preset Drive packs are .fxp for Serum 1.3 and later, including Serum 2. Make sure you are dropping them into your Serum presets folder, not your samples folder.

FAQ

Do I need a paid version of Serum to make a Reese bass?

You need a licensed copy of Xfer Serum. The technique in this guide works in Serum 1.3 and later, including Serum 2.

How long does it take to make a Reese bass in Serum?

Around ten to fifteen minutes once you know the steps. Loading a ready-made preset takes seconds.

Can I make a Reese without two oscillators?

You can fake it with unison detune on one oscillator, but the classic Reese movement comes from two saws beating against each other, so two oscillators give the truest sound.
