Reese Bass vs Growl Bass: Which Serum Sound Should You Learn First? (2026)

Every producer who gets into drum and bass or neurofunk hits the same fork in the road. You hear two kinds of heavy bass and you want both: the thick, churning Reese that anchors a track, and the snarling, talking growl that dominates a drop. They are related, they are both built in Xfer Serum, and they get confused constantly. So which one should you actually learn first, and what is the real difference?

This is an honest, side-by-side comparison. A Reese is the foundation. A growl is what you get when you push a Reese much further. Knowing which to focus on first will save you weeks of frustration. By the end of this post you will know exactly which sound to learn, in what order, and how to get both fast if you would rather skip ahead.

Quick verdict

  • Learn first: Reese bass. It is the foundation every growl is built on, and it is achievable in your first week of sound design.
  • Learn next: Growl bass. It is the advanced payoff, built by resampling and processing a Reese until it snarls.
  • Best for fast results: ready-made presets that ship both, so you can study finished examples while you learn.
  • Skip if: you only make melodic, clean styles where neither aggressive bass fits.

What a Reese bass is

A Reese is the originating bass sound of drum and bass and neurofunk. It is built from two (or more) sawtooth oscillators detuned slightly against each other so they beat and create slow, organic movement. Run that through a filter, drop it into bass range, add a clean sub, and you have the churning, wide low end that sits under most serious DnB tracks.

The Reese is the foundation sound. It is relatively quick to build, forgiving to mix, and once you understand it you understand the bones of the entire genre. If you are starting out, this is the sound to master first.

What a growl bass is

A growl bass (often called a neuro growl) is what a Reese becomes after you push it hard. You take that moving Reese, then resample it, add FM for metallic harmonics, sweep narrow band-pass filters for a vocal quality, and stack distortion until the bass snarls and talks. The result is the aggressive, designed, almost vocal bass that owns a neurofunk or heavy dubstep drop.

A growl is advanced. It takes more steps, more processing, and usually more than one pass of resampling. It is the sound that makes people ask how you did that, but it is not where a beginner should start, because a weak Reese makes a weak growl.

Deep dive: difficulty and learning curve

The single biggest reason to learn the Reese first is that the growl is literally built on top of it. If you cannot make a solid, moving Reese, your resampled growl will inherit every weakness of the source. Beginners who jump straight to growls usually end up with a thin, harsh mess, because they skipped the foundation.

A Reese, by contrast, is achievable early. Two detuned saws, a filter, an LFO, a clean sub, and you have a usable bass. You can write whole tracks on Reese basses alone while you build the skills for growls.

Winner: Reese bass. It is the right first goal, and it unlocks the growl later.

Deep dive: the role each plays in a track

These two sounds are not competing for the same job. A Reese is the bass that holds a section together, the moving low end you can leave running under a groove. A growl is a feature: the snarling lead bass that dominates a drop and then steps back.

Most strong tracks use both. The Reese carries the energy, the growl provides the moments. Understanding that they serve different roles is more useful than treating it as a contest.

Winner: tie. They do different jobs, and a complete producer uses both.

Deep dive: how the growl is built from the Reese

This is the part that confuses people, so it is worth being precise. The path from Reese to growl looks like this:

  • Build a strong Reese (detuned saws, filter, sub).
  • Add band-pass filter sweeps for a vocal, formant quality.
  • Add FM for metallic harmonics.
  • Resample the result into audio and load it back into Serum.
  • Re-modulate and distort the resampled sound.
  • Optionally resample again for more density.

Every one of those steps assumes a good Reese underneath. That is why the learning order matters: Reese first, then the processing chain that turns it into a growl. Follow the full builds in the Reese guide and the neuro growl walkthrough.

Winner: Reese bass as the starting point, because it is the prerequisite for everything else.

Pros and cons

Reese bass

  • Beginner-friendly and quick to build
  • The foundation of the whole DnB and neuro sound
  • More forgiving to mix
  • Works across DnB, neuro and dubstep
  • Can sound static if you skip filter motion

Growl bass

  • The aggressive, attention-grabbing drop sound
  • Hugely expressive once mastered
  • Built directly on Reese skills, so it rewards a strong foundation
  • Easy to make harsh or thin if the source Reese is weak
  • Takes longer and demands resampling

Who should choose each

Learn the Reese first if you:

  • Are new to bass sound design
  • Want a usable bass you can build tracks on this week
  • Produce DnB, neuro or dubstep at any level

Move on to the growl when you:

  • Can already build a solid, moving Reese
  • Want the snarling lead bass that owns a drop
  • Are comfortable resampling audio back into Serum

The verdict

For most producers, the answer is clear: learn the Reese bass first. It is the foundation, it is achievable early, and every growl you ever make will be built on it. Once your Reese is solid, the growl becomes the natural next step rather than a frustrating wall. The two are not rivals, they are stages of the same journey.

FAQ

Is a growl bass just a Reese bass?

No, but it starts as one. A growl is a Reese that has been resampled, FM-processed and distorted until it snarls and talks. The Reese is the foundation, the growl is the advanced result.

Which is harder to make, a Reese or a growl?

The growl is harder. It involves resampling, FM and heavy processing, all built on a solid Reese. The Reese itself is beginner-friendly.

Should a beginner learn the Reese or the growl first?

The Reese, every time. It is the prerequisite for the growl and is achievable in your first week of sound design.
